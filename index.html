Your Guide to Safe Cyber Behaviors

Your cybersecurity starts from your safe practices and habits!

Five cyber practices that help keep you safe across online services and digital environments.
Learn why each practice matters, what are practical advice to apply it, and common mistakes to avoid.

Using Strong Passwords

Why it matters

  • Passwords prevent outsiders from accessing your online accounts.
  • Strong passwords protect your accounts.

Practical advice

  • Use a long rather than a complex password.
  • A strong password is made up of three random words e.g., Cup Fish Baraccuda.
  • If the three random words don’t meet website requirements, you can start the words with an upper-case letter and then append your favourite number and “?”.
  • Choose passwords that are easy for you to remember but unusual and hard for others to guess.
  • Use a different password for each of your online accounts.
  • Do not share your passwords with others or leave them where others can see them.

Common mistakes

  • Using guessable passwords like your birthdate, phone number, or pet name as a password.
  • Reusing the same password on all your accounts.

Not Falling for a Phishing Scam (Phishing Prevention)

Why it matters

  • You can protect yourself from data theft and device compromise.
  • You can protect yourself from fraud.

Practical advice

    (If the message doesn’t pass these tests, don’t click)

  • Slow down for 5 seconds before clicking links to look for red flags in the language used (rushing/scaring/ rewards/ urgency/emotional language).
  • Verify the sender when you receive a message. Ask yourself: (Do you know him/her?, Did you expect this message?, Can you contact the sender to check using a different channel (e.g., SMS, WhatsApp)?).
  • Do not provide your personal information (e.g., name, birthdate, ID, passwords, OTP, bank details), just because someone is asking.
  • If a message seems to be from an official entity, visit their official website and consider contacting them via that website to confirm.
  • Still not sure?, You can use trusted online security tools like iBarry security platform (https://www.ibarry.ch/en/security-checks/ ) or VirusTotal ( https://www.virustotal.com/gui/home/upload ) to verify links in messages.
  • You can also use the official reporting service offered by the UK (NCSC) to report suspicious messages via report@phishing.gov.uk or by texting 7726.
  • Already clicked on a suspicious link or think you have been hacked?, Report it to the service provider and to https://www.reportfraud.police.uk/ . if you are in England, Wales or Northern Ireland call 0300 123 2040 to report . If you are in Scotland, report to Police Scotland by calling 101.

Common mistakes

  • Rushing to act on messages due to urgency or fear.
  • Clicking on links in messages from unknown sources and disclosing personal information.

Using Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Why it matters

  • 2FA requires you to provide your password and then to engage in an extra step: e.g., providing a security code that is texted to your smartphone, or via an Authenticator app that you install.
  • • 2FA gives you extra protection in case your password is guessed or stolen.

Practical advice

  • Use 2FA for any accounts where it is offered, especially high-value accounts (e.g., email, banking, government services). Check in the account’s security & privacy settings to enable it.
  • Most service providers provide step-by-step (2FA adding) instructions for you to follow, review and follow them.
  • You might be asked to install and use an Authenticator app (e.g., Google Authenticator or Microsoft Authenticator) to generate a one-time code when signing in, follow the required steps and install these apps.

Common mistakes

  • Giving your 2FA code to a hacker or untrusted person.

Backing Up Important Data

Why it matters

  • A backup is a copy of your important data so you can get your files back in case your computer dies or is hacked.

Practical advice

  • Back up your important data and files regularly using Cloud storage (e.g., Apple iCloud, Google Drive, or Microsoft OneDrive), or removable media (like an external hard drive) or both.
  • When you choose to back up your data, find and follow the relevant backup instructions on the provider’s official website.
  • Test your backups regularly and make sure they are restorable and haven’t been corrupted in some way.

Common mistakes

  • Not making backups.

Updating System, Software & Apps

Why it matters

  • Updates are more secure versions of software that fix security vulnerabilities and enhance performance.

Practical advice

  • Option 1: Turn on 'automatic updates' in your device's settings. This takes all the effort out of keeping your system up to date.
  • Option 2: Install updates as soon as they are released.
  • Always install updates only from trusted and official sources.
  • Avoid using devices that no longer receive updates (Update support for them has been discontinued).

Common mistakes

  • Delaying or ignoring system/app updates.
